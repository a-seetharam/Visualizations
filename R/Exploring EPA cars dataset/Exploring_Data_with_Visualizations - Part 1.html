Preface: The automobile industry is one of the largest in the world. Growth of automobile sales can indicate the overall state of the economy, the health of commodities and labour markets, and in can be a leading indicator of practical applications of cutting-edge technologies. The US automotive market was, until recently, the largest for the global industry (now China being the largest). The US auto market is still arguably the best in terms of technology, infrastructure and supply chain integration. One of the remarkable things about the US auto market is the quantum of data thats available. The information ecosystem in the US is robust and unparalleled across the world and provides many opportunities to learn from data. Lets use an EPA dataset to unlock information with visualizations.

The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) collects and documents various attributes from the automotive sector. Lets use this data as an example to illustrate how we can learn more about cars and the US automotive industry.

The data can be accessed from https://www.fueleconomy.gov/feg/ws/index.shtml#vehicle A copy of the zip file can also be accessed from this repo (vehicles.csv.zip).

For most buyers from the middle class and lower middle, a cars fuel economy is a key factor in the purchase decision. Cars with higher fuel economy tend to attract more buyers from these segments. So how has the fuel economy changed over the years?

mpgByYr <- ddply(cars, ~year, summarise, avgMPG =  
                   mean(comb08), avgHghy = mean(highway08), avgCity =  
                   mean(city08))

ggplot(mpgByYr, aes(year, avgMPG)) + geom_point() +  
  geom_smooth() + xlab("Year") + ylab("Average MPG") +  
  ggtitle("All cars")
## `geom_smooth()` using method = 'loess'

We observe that the average fuel economy of all the cars has increased markedly from around 2006-07. It makes sense to see that auto manufacturers placed an emphasis on fuel economy because the global oil prices were rising at a record rate between 2004-2008 (global oil price hit a record high of $147.30 in July 2008).

Since mileage data is not being collected on a passenger-mile basis and categorized by fuel, in this data structure, the increase this could be due to the averaging effect produced by higher number of hybrid (e.g.Prius, Insight), electric (e.g.Tesla) cars and diesel engined cars, which can have effectively higher MPG as compared to regular gasoline cars. Lets see if the gas powered cars have improved in terms of fuel economy.

Lets separate the run-of-the-mill gasoline powered cars from the others.

gasCars <- subset(cars, fuelType1 %in% c("Regular  
Gasoline", "Premium Gasoline", "Midgrade Gasoline") &  
                    fuelType2 == "" & atvType != "Hybrid")

The combined highway/urban fuel economy is listed in feature combo08. How has the average mileage changed over the years for gasoline powered cars? Lets plot and see.

mpgByYr_Gas <- ddply(gasCars, ~year, summarise, avgMPG =  
                       mean(comb08))

ggplot(mpgByYr_Gas, aes(year, avgMPG)) + geom_point() +  
  geom_smooth() + xlab("Year") + ylab("Average MPG") +  
  ggtitle("Gasoline cars")
## `geom_smooth()` using method = 'loess'

We see that gasoline powered cars have been improving in terms of fuel economy for about the last two decades; the average fuel economy has increased significantly since the end of 2010. This would partly be due to a stagflation effect seen in the automotive market, which is the combined impact of higher oil prices and lower ability to afford cars due to a recently onset recession. Improvements in technology is also reflected in increase in fuel economy.

A cars power output is typically proportional to its engine displacement and the fuel economy is inversely proportional to the displacement. (Its typical in most cases, but there are exceptions.)

We understand that engine displacement and fuel economy are inversely related. So how has the average displacement changed over the years?

avgCarSize <- ddply(gasCars, ~year, summarise, avgDispl =  mean(displ))

ggplot(avgCarSize, aes(year, avgDispl)) + geom_point() +  
  geom_smooth() + xlab("Year") + ylab("Average engine displacement (l)")
## `geom_smooth()` using method = 'loess'

We can see that the average engine displacement has an almost inverted U-shape. It increased between from around 1985 for about a decade, and somewhat plateaued for almost a decade after 1995. The trend in recent years has been an overall decrease.
